**1. The Grid**

```Python
plt.grid(axis="y", linewidth=2, color="lightgray", linestyle="dashed")
```

- **`axis="y"`**: This tells Matplotlib to only draw horizontal grid lines (across the y-axis). If you wanted vertical lines, you would use `"x"`. For both, use `"both"`.
    
- **`linewidth=2`**: Makes the grid lines thicker than the default (which is usually 1).
    
- **`color="lightgray"`**: Sets the color of the grid lines so they don't overpower the actual data.
    
- **`linestyle="dashed"`**: Changes the line from solid to dashed (you can also use `'--'` as a shortcut, or `':'` for dotted).
    

**2. Title and Axis Labels**

Python

```Python
plt.title("students data", fontsize=25, fontweight="bold", color="red")
plt.xlabel("year", fontsize=25, fontweight="bold", color="red")
plt.ylabel("year", fontsize=25, fontweight="bold", color="red")
```

- **`plt.title()`**: Places a main heading at the very top of the chart.
    
- **`plt.xlabel()` / `plt.ylabel()`**: Labels the horizontal (x) and vertical (y) axes so the viewer knows what the numbers represent.
    
- **Text Formatting**: For all three of these, you used `fontsize=25` to make the text quite large, `fontweight="bold"` to make the letters thick, and `color="red"`.
    
- _Note on logic:_ You labeled both the x-axis and y-axis as "year". Usually, in a "students data" plot, the x-axis is "Year" and the y-axis is "Number of Students".
    

**3. Tick Marks and Values**

Python

```Python
plt.xticks(year_x)
plt.tick_params(axis="both", colors="red")
```

- **`plt.xticks(year_x)`**: This forces the x-axis to specifically display the values inside the `year_x` variable (which is likely a list, like `[2021, 2022, 2023]`). By default, Matplotlib guesses where to put the numbers; this command gives you exact control.
    
- **`plt.tick_params(...)`**: This styles the little lines (ticks) and the numbers next to them. `axis="both"` applies your styling to both the x and y axes, and `colors="red"` turns the numbers red.
